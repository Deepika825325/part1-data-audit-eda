# Business Memo: Customer Churn Risk Assessment

## Executive Summary

An exploratory analysis was conducted to identify customer behaviours associated with future churn and to highlight areas requiring attention before launching retention campaigns.

The analysis found that customer inactivity, campaign engagement, purchase frequency, loyalty participation, and customer value are the strongest indicators of churn risk. Approximately **40.93% of total analysed revenue (₹2.50 million)** is associated with customers who eventually churn, indicating a significant revenue preservation opportunity.

Four priority retention cohorts were identified, including **1,424 customers with no recent campaign engagement**, **696 low-frequency purchasers**, **481 highly inactive customers**, and **269 customers exhibiting high return rates**. These segments should be prioritized based on both churn risk and business impact.

---

# Key Findings

## 1. Customer Inactivity Is the Strongest Churn Signal

Customer inactivity emerged as the most powerful indicator of future churn.

| Customer Activity Segment | Churn Rate |
| ------------------------- | ---------: |
| Recently Active           |     18.71% |
| Moderately Inactive       |     50.00% |
| Highly Inactive           |     88.36% |

Customers who stop visiting the platform become substantially more likely to churn, suggesting that disengagement begins well before purchasing activity ceases.

---

## 2. Low Purchase Activity Is Associated with Higher Churn Risk

Retained customers placed an average of **5.18 orders**, compared with **3.04 orders** among churned customers.

Declining purchase frequency appears to be an important early warning signal and may indicate weakening customer relationships.

---

## 3. Campaign Engagement Supports Retention

Customers who interacted with marketing campaigns demonstrated stronger retention behaviour.

| Customer Segment | Churn Rate |
| ---------------- | ---------: |
| Campaign Engaged |     42.32% |
| Not Engaged      |     50.14% |

Customers who engaged with marketing campaigns exhibited a materially lower churn rate than customers who did not engage (42.32% vs 50.14%). This suggests that campaign interaction is not only an engagement metric but also an early indicator of customer retention.

---

## 4. Loyalty Program Performance Requires Review

Higher-tier loyalty members exhibited stronger retention outcomes.

| Loyalty Tier | Churn Rate |
| ------------ | ---------: |
| Platinum     |     37.14% |
| Gold         |     40.75% |
| Not Enrolled |     48.34% |
| Silver       |     48.81% |

Gold and Platinum members demonstrated meaningfully lower churn rates than other customers. However, a notable finding is that **Silver-tier customers churn slightly more frequently than customers who are not enrolled in the loyalty program**.

This suggests that the Silver tier may not be delivering sufficient value to influence customer retention. While higher loyalty tiers appear effective, the entry-level tier may require redesign, stronger incentives, or a clearer progression path toward Gold membership.

---

## 5. Revenue Exposure from Churn Is Significant

The analysis identified substantial revenue concentration among customers who eventually churn.

| Metric                         |         Value |
| ------------------------------ | ------------: |
| Total Revenue Analysed         | ₹6.12 Million |
| Revenue from Churned Customers | ₹2.50 Million |
| Revenue Exposure               |        40.93% |

Reducing churn among at-risk customers represents a meaningful revenue preservation opportunity.

---

# Priority Retention Cohorts

The following customer groups should be prioritized before broad retention campaigns are launched.

| Priority | Cohort                            | Customers | Recommended Timeline     |
| -------- | --------------------------------- | --------: | ------------------------ |
| 1        | High Inactivity Customers         |       481 | Immediate (0–30 Days)    |
| 2        | Low Campaign Engagement Customers |     1,424 | Short Term (30–60 Days)  |
| 3        | Low Purchase Frequency Customers  |       696 | Medium Term (60–90 Days) |
| 4        | High Return Rate Customers        |       269 | Ongoing Monitoring       |

### Why Start with Inactive Customers?

Highly inactive customers exhibit an **88.36% churn rate**, making them the highest-risk segment identified in the analysis.

This cohort contains **481 customers**. Assuming average revenue patterns remain consistent, reactivating even a small proportion of these customers could preserve meaningful revenue that would otherwise be lost through churn. As a result, inactivity reduction should receive the highest retention priority.

---

# Recommended Business Priorities

## Priority 1: Immediate Inactivity Intervention (0–30 Days)

**Target Segment:** 481 highly inactive customers

Actions:

* Trigger automated re-engagement campaigns.
* Deliver personalized product recommendations.
* Launch inactivity-based email and push notification journeys.
* Monitor customers with rapidly increasing days since last visit.

---

## Priority 2: Improve Campaign Engagement (30–60 Days)

**Target Segment:** 1,424 customers with no recent campaign engagement

Actions:

* Personalize campaign content and offers.
* Improve targeting and message relevance.
* Test alternative communication channels and delivery times.
* Identify customers repeatedly ignoring campaign communications.

---

## Priority 3: Optimize Loyalty Program Effectiveness (30–90 Days)

**Target Segment:** Silver-tier members and non-enrolled customers

Actions:

* Review Silver-tier reward structure.
* Introduce stronger early-stage membership benefits.
* Create clearer progression incentives toward Gold membership.
* Evaluate whether current loyalty benefits meaningfully influence customer behaviour.

---

## Priority 4: Protect High-Value Customers

**Definition:** Customers within the top 25% of total customer spend.

**Segment Size:** 600 customers

**Average Revenue per Customer:** ₹5,446

Although high-value customers churn less frequently (35.17%) than regular customers (50.89%), they contribute a disproportionate share of business revenue.

Actions:

* Establish proactive monitoring for behavioural changes.
* Prioritize personalized retention outreach.
* Trigger alerts when high-value customers exhibit inactivity signals.
* Provide loyalty and service experiences tailored to customer value.

---

# Conclusion

Customer inactivity emerged as the strongest predictor of future churn, followed by campaign engagement, purchase frequency, loyalty participation, and customer value.

The analysis also revealed two particularly important business opportunities:

1. Highly inactive customers represent the most urgent retention segment due to their exceptionally high churn risk.
2. The Silver loyalty tier appears to underperform relative to expectations, suggesting that improvements to the entry-level loyalty experience may provide retention benefits beyond traditional marketing interventions.

Given that **40.93% of analysed revenue** is associated with customers who eventually churn, targeted retention efforts focused on inactivity reduction, campaign engagement, and loyalty optimization have the potential to generate meaningful business impact.

These findings provide a practical roadmap for customer retention planning and establish a strong analytical foundation for future churn prediction modelling.
