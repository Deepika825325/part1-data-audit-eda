# Business Memo

## Subject: Key Churn Risks and Retention Priorities Identified from Customer Analysis

### Background

A review was conducted across customer profiles, purchase history, support interactions, website activity, campaign exposure, and churn outcomes to identify the factors most strongly associated with customer attrition.

The analysis covered 2,400 customers and found an overall churn rate of **46.96%**, indicating that nearly half of the customer base becomes inactive within the next 60 days.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Declining Customer Engagement Is the Strongest Churn Signal

Customers who churned showed significantly lower platform engagement than retained customers.

| Metric                | Retained | Churned |
| --------------------- | -------: | ------: |
| Sessions (30 Days)    |     6.73 |    4.02 |
| Days Since Last Visit |     9.77 |   26.55 |

Customers who stopped visiting the platform were substantially more likely to churn. This suggests that disengagement begins well before a customer is officially lost.

### 2. Low Purchase Activity Is Closely Linked to Churn

Retained customers placed an average of **5.18 orders**, compared with **3.04 orders** among churned customers.

Purchase frequency appears to be one of the strongest indicators of customer loyalty. Customers with infrequent purchases should be considered a high-priority retention segment.

### 3. Campaign Engagement Matters

Customers who remained active interacted with marketing campaigns more frequently than customers who churned.

| Metric          | Retained | Churned |
| --------------- | -------: | ------: |
| Campaign Clicks |     0.74 |    0.55 |

This suggests that marketing engagement can serve as an early signal of customer health and should be monitored continuously.

### 4. Product Returns Contribute to Retention Risk

Customers who churned exhibited higher return rates than retained customers.

Although the difference was smaller than engagement-related metrics, repeated returns may indicate dissatisfaction with products, fulfillment quality, or customer expectations.

### 5. Premium Loyalty Membership Appears Beneficial

Customers in Gold and Platinum loyalty tiers demonstrated lower churn rates than non-members.

However, Silver-tier customers performed similarly to customers who were not enrolled in the loyalty program, suggesting that entry-level benefits may not be sufficiently compelling.

---

## Areas With Limited Impact

The analysis found little evidence that the following factors meaningfully influence churn:

* Support ticket sentiment
* Ticket reopen rates

While these metrics remain important for customer service monitoring, they are not currently among the strongest retention indicators.

---

## Recommended Business Priorities

### Priority 1: Build an Inactivity Monitoring Program

Customers who have not visited the platform recently should be flagged automatically.

Recommended actions:

* Re-engagement emails
* Personalized offers
* Product recommendations
* Cart recovery campaigns

### Priority 2: Target Low-Frequency Buyers

Customers with declining purchase frequency should receive retention-focused interventions before they become inactive.

Recommended actions:

* Loyalty incentives
* Repeat-purchase reminders
* Personalized discounts
* Category-specific recommendations

### Priority 3: Improve Loyalty Program Effectiveness

The loyalty program appears effective for higher-tier members but less effective at the Silver level.

Recommended actions:

* Review Silver-tier benefits
* Create faster progression paths toward Gold membership
* Increase perceived value of membership rewards

### Priority 4: Use Engagement Metrics as Early Warning Signals

Customer engagement metrics should become part of ongoing retention monitoring.

Key metrics to track:

* Sessions in the last 30 days
* Days since last visit
* Campaign click activity
* Purchase frequency

---

## Conclusion

The analysis indicates that customer churn is primarily driven by declining engagement rather than customer service interactions.

The strongest risk signals observed were:

1. Increased inactivity
2. Lower session activity
3. Reduced purchase frequency
4. Low campaign engagement

Retention efforts should focus on identifying customers early in the disengagement cycle and intervening before purchasing behavior declines further.
